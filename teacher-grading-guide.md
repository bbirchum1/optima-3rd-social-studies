# Teacher Grading Guide - Grade 3 Social Studies

This guide is based on the Assignment tab content extracted from the HTML lesson files in this course folder. It skips generic Canvas submission directions and focuses on gradable student work.

For recurring Curator Chronicle prompts, grade for a complete, lesson-connected sentence with a clear "because" explanation. Do not require identical wording to the samples below.

## 1. What Historians Study
Filename: `ss-3-01.01-what-historians-study.html`

- **Q1: Two fields of social studies:** Expected: any two of history, geography, civics/government, and economics. History studies the past; geography studies places, maps, land, and how people use them; civics/government studies rules, rights, responsibilities, and community decisions; economics studies resources, choices, trade, goods/services, money, buyers, and sellers.
- **Q2: Why learn about the past:** Sample: Learning about the past helps people understand how communities changed, why people made certain choices, and what evidence tells us about earlier lives.
- **Curator Task: First Notebook Entry:** Look for 2-3 sentences explaining that social studies studies people, places, the past, government/citizenship, and choices/resources. Strong answers explain that museum curators need all four fields to tell a complete, accurate story.
- **Curator Chronicle:** Answers vary; look for one complete sentence naming a lesson idea and a because-reason.

## 2. Primary Secondary Sources
Filename: `ss-3-01.02-primary-secondary-sources.html`

- **Q1: Primary vs. secondary sources:** Expected: A primary source comes from the time being studied, such as a photograph, letter, map, diary, artifact, or document. A secondary source explains or retells information later, such as a textbook, article, or museum label.
- **Q2: Why study primary sources:** Sample: Curators study primary sources because they give direct evidence from the past. Textbooks can help explain, but primary sources let curators notice details and make evidence-based claims.
- **Curator Task: Study a New Source:** Answers vary because the source image may vary. Look for clear observations of visible details, a reasonable inference about what the source shows about past life, and an explanation of why the source helps people understand history. Do not require facts not visible or taught.
- **Curator Chronicle:** Answers vary; look for a sentence explaining that primary and secondary sources help curators study the past.

## 3. Asking Questions
Filename: `ss-3-01.03-asking-questions.html`

- **Q1: Two question types:** Expected: any two valid historian question types from the lesson, such as observation, context, evidence, connection, and significance. The student should explain what each helps them do.
- **Q2: Why ask before labeling:** Sample: Asking questions first keeps a curator from guessing. Good questions help the curator observe, check evidence, and write a more accurate museum label.
- **Curator Task: Five Questions:** Look for one question for each type: observation, context, evidence, connection, and significance. Questions should fit an old compass from the 1800s and should help prepare a museum label.
- **Curator Chronicle:** Answers vary; look for a complete sentence about how questions help curators understand evidence.

## 4. Technology Reliable Info
Filename: `ss-3-01.04-technology-reliable-info.html`

- **Q1: Reliable vs. unreliable sources:** Expected: A reliable source has a trustworthy author or organization, current or clear date, and evidence/sources. An unreliable source may have no author, no evidence, old or unclear information, exaggerated wording, or clickbait.
- **Q2: Three-Check Test:** Expected: WHO made it, WHEN it was made/updated, and what EVIDENCE it gives. It helps curators choose trustworthy information before using it in an exhibit.
- **Curator Task: Evaluate Search Results:** Expected: Source A, Florida Memory/State Library, is more reliable. Look for the Three-Check Test: state library/identified author, updated date, and photographs/primary source excerpts. Source B is weaker because it lacks an author, is older, uses sensational wording, and cites no evidence.
- **Curator Chronicle:** Answers vary; look for a sentence about checking sources before trusting information.

## 5. Word Roots
Filename: `ss-3-01.05-word-roots.html`

- **Q1: Two word roots explained:** Expected: any two roots from the lesson with correct meanings and connections. Common examples: hist/story or inquiry connects to history; geo/earth connects to geography; civ/citizen connects to civics; eco/household or management connects to economics.
- **Q2: Why roots help curators:** Sample: Word roots help curators understand unfamiliar academic words and explain them to visitors. For example, geo helps a visitor remember that geography has to do with Earth and places.
- **Curator Task: Word Root Notebook Entry:** Look for 3-4 sentences naming the four fields and explaining how their roots reveal what each field studies. Strong answers connect roots to museum work, not just list definitions.
- **Curator Chronicle:** Answers vary; look for a sentence about roots helping explain social studies vocabulary.

- **Q2: Question types and why they matter:** Expected: any two valid question types, plus an explanation that questions prevent guessing and help a curator observe, check, connect, and explain evidence.

## 8. Navigation Tools And Explorer Routes
Filename: `ss-3-02.02-navigation-tools-and-explorer-routes.html`

- **Q1: Navigation tool and purpose:** Expected: compass helped sailors know direction; astrolabe helped estimate latitude/position using the sun or stars; maps/charts helped plan routes and record coastlines.
- **Q2: Why maps improved:** Expected: maps improved as explorers and sailors gathered new information, recorded routes/coastlines, and shared observations from voyages.
- **Q3: Tools and courage:** Sample: Tools reduced some danger but did not remove risk. The need for compasses, astrolabes, and maps shows explorers faced uncertainty, distance, and the possibility of getting lost.
- **Curator Task: Navigation Tools Display Label:** Look for 3-4 sentences identifying the mariner astrolabe as a navigation tool, explaining that sailors used it with the sky to help find location/latitude, and explaining why it mattered for ocean travel.
- **Curator Chronicle:** Answers vary; look for a sentence about navigation tools helping explorers travel more safely or accurately.

## 11. Consequences Of Exploration
Filename: `ss-3-02.05-consequences-of-exploration.html`

- **Q1: Columbian Exchange effect:** Expected: foods, animals, plants, people, and diseases moved across the Atlantic. Accept examples such as horses, cattle, wheat, sugar, potatoes, corn, tomatoes, or diseases, with an explanation of how daily life changed.

## 14. Cardinal And Intermediate Directions
Filename: `ss-3-03.02-cardinal-and-intermediate-directions.html`

- **Q1: Eight directions:** Expected: cardinal directions are north, south, east, west. Intermediate directions are northeast, northwest, southeast, southwest. Intermediate means between the main/cardinal directions.
- **Q2: Why eight directions:** Sample: Eight directions give more precise locations. For example, if a place is between north and east, northeast is clearer than saying only north or east.
- **Q3: Directions and courtesy:** Sample: Clear directions help visitors find things and avoid confusion, which is courteous because it respects and helps others.
- **Curator Task: Direction Guide:** Answers vary. Look for 2-3 sentences using direction words accurately to describe locations in a familiar or imaginary place.
- **Curator Chronicle:** Answers vary; look for a sentence about directions helping map readers.

## 15. Physical Political Elevation Population Maps
Filename: `ss-3-03.03-physical-political-elevation-population-maps.html`

- **Q1: Two map types:** Expected examples: physical maps show landforms/water; political maps show boundaries/countries/states/cities; elevation maps show height of land; population maps show where people live or density. Each should include a matching question.
- **Q2: Why different maps matter:** Expected: one map cannot answer every question because maps are designed for different purposes. A museum needs different maps for land, borders, height, and people.
- **Curator Task: Map Recommendation:** Expected: recommend an elevation or physical/elevation map for biggest mountains in South America, with elevation best if the question is about height. Explain why political or population maps would be less useful.

## 16. Map Scale And Measuring Distance
Filename: `ss-3-03.04-map-scale-and-measuring-distance.html`

- **Q1: Scale bar:** Expected: a scale bar shows how distance on the map matches real distance. Without it, readers cannot tell how far apart places really are.
- **Q2: Scale practice:** Expected: 600 miles. Reason: 1 inch = 200 miles, so 3 inches = 3 x 200 = 600 miles.
- **Q3: Scale and explorer routes:** Sample: A scale bar helps visitors understand how far explorers traveled, not just where the route line goes. Distance shows risk, time, and difficulty.
- **Curator Task: Scale Bar Explanation Card:** Look for 2-3 clear visitor-facing sentences explaining what the scale shows, how to use it, and why real distance matters.
- **Curator Chronicle:** Answers vary; look for a sentence about scale connecting map distance to real distance.

## 17. Maps And Globes Understanding Distortion
Filename: `ss-3-03.05-maps-and-globes-understanding-distortion.html`

- **Q1: Distortion:** Expected: distortion is when shapes, sizes, distances, or directions are stretched/changed. Flat maps have distortion because Earth is round and cannot be flattened perfectly.
- **Q2: Globe vs. flat map:** Expected: a globe shows Earth shape/relative sizes more accurately; a flat map is easier to carry, print, compare, write on, or show the whole world at once.
- **Curator Task: Distortion Explanation:** Look for 2-3 sentences explaining distortion, why Greenland may look stretched/too large on some flat maps, and when a globe or flat map is useful.
- **Curator Chronicle:** Answers vary; look for a sentence about maps/globes each having strengths and limits.
